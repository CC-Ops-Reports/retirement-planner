🔻 Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) — the deadline you can't skip

Once money sits in a pre-tax account (Traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b)), the IRS eventually forces you to pull it out and pay tax — that's an RMD.

  • His start age is 75 (anyone born 1960 or later; it's 73 for 1951–1959).
  • Each year's amount = last year's ending balance ÷ an IRS life-expectancy factor (24.6 at age 75, shrinking yearly — so RMDs grow).
  • The very first one can wait until April 1 of the year after he turns 75 — but doubling up two RMDs in one year can spike his bracket, so usually take the first on time.
  • Roth IRAs have no RMDs for him or your mom — money there grows untouched for life. (That's a core reason to convert.)
  • Miss one and the penalty is steep (25%, or 10% if fixed quickly). Don't miss it.
  • QCD trick: from 70½ he can send up to ~$108,000/yr directly from the IRA to charity — it counts toward the RMD and is never taxed.

🔄 Roth conversions — the single biggest lever he has right now

A conversion moves money from pre-tax to Roth. He pays ordinary income tax on the amount this year, and in exchange that money never gets taxed again and never has an RMD.

  • His golden window is right now — roughly 60 to 75: he's retired (low income), not yet on Social Security, and RMDs haven't started. That's when conversions are cheapest.
  • Fill the low brackets: convert just enough each year to reach the top of the 12% or 22% bracket (the Explorer above shows exactly how much room he has). Spreading it over several years beats one big conversion.
  • The "widow's penalty": when one spouse passes, the survivor files single — brackets roughly halve, so the same income is taxed much higher. Converting now, while both file jointly, locks in today's lower rates.
  • It can't be undone — conversions are permanent (no do-overs since 2018), so size them deliberately.
  • Pay the tax from outside the IRA (from the brokerage/cash), so 100% of the converted dollars keep growing in the Roth.
  • At 75+, he must take his RMD first; only what's left above the RMD can be converted.
🪜 Which account to spend first — withdrawal order

The classic order is taxable → pre-tax → Roth, saving the tax-free Roth for last. But the smarter move blends them:

  • In low-income years, deliberately pull (or convert) from pre-tax up to the top of a low bracket — even if he doesn't need the cash — to drain it before RMDs force bigger taxable withdrawals later.
  • Use taxable-account sales to fund living costs while keeping taxable income low (and tap the 0% capital-gains bracket — see below).
  • Let Roth grow as long as possible; it's the best account to leave to heirs (tax-free, and they get 10 years to drain it).
🗓️ Social Security — when to turn it on
  • Every year he waits past Full Retirement Age (67) adds ~8% to the check, up to age 70 — a guaranteed, inflation-adjusted raise that's hard to beat in the market.
  • Delaying also maximizes the survivor benefit — the higher earner's benefit is what the surviving spouse keeps for life.
  • Those 60–70 gap years (before he claims) are prime conversion years — Social Security isn't there yet inflating his income.
  • Tax torpedo: other income can drag up to 85% of his Social Security into taxation. The thresholds ($32k/$44k married) aren't inflation-adjusted, so more gets taxed over time — another reason to shrink the pre-tax balance early.
💚 The 0% capital-gains bracket — free money for early retirees

Long-term capital gains and qualified dividends are taxed at 0% as long as total taxable income stays under about $96,700 (married, 2025). In his low-income years he can sell appreciated investments and pay zero federal tax, then rebuy to reset his cost basis.

The catch: Roth conversions and 0% gain-harvesting draw from the same low-bracket space. Each year he chooses which to prioritize — the Explorer shows how much room he has.

🏥 IRMAA — the Medicare premium surcharge to dodge
  • Once on Medicare (65), higher income raises his Part B & D premiums — the surcharge is called IRMAA.
  • It works on cliffs: going $1 over a tier triggers the whole surcharge (hundreds to thousands per person per year).
  • It uses a 2-year lookback — his 2026 premiums are based on 2024 income — so conversions at 63–64 can quietly raise premiums at 65–66. Plan around the tiers.
🩺 Before 65 — health insurance & the ACA subsidy trap

He's retired at 60 but not yet on Medicare (65). If he buys coverage on the ACA marketplace, the premium subsidy is based on his income (MAGI). A big Roth conversion or withdrawal can wipe out thousands in subsidies. Until 65, there's a real tension: convert aggressively vs. keep income low for cheap health insurance. Often it's worth keeping income modest until Medicare, then converting harder from 65–75.

🌴 South Carolina — why his state taxes are low
  • Social Security is 100% state-tax-free in SC.
  • At 65+, each spouse gets a $10,000 retirement-income deduction plus a $15,000 age deduction — sheltering a big chunk of IRA/pension withdrawals from state tax.
  • Under 65, the retirement deduction is smaller ($3,000 each) — so SC tax on conversions is a touch higher now and drops at 65.
  • SC's top rate (~6.2% and slowly falling) only hits income above ~$17,800; the first chunk is 0–3%.

  • Federal brackets: 10 / 12 / 22 / 24 / 32 / 35 / 37%
  • Standard deduction (married): ~$30,000 + senior add-ons at 65+
  • 0% capital-gains income ceiling (married): ~$96,700
  • Social Security taxation thresholds (married): $32,000 / $44,000
  • QCD limit: ~$108,000/yr, from age 70½
